How machine learning runs the case journey

The Verifications Decision Engine transforms verification from a sequence of disconnected attempts into an intelligent operation—one that anticipates where each case is heading, directs the strongest path forward, and adapts as the evidence changes.

Most automation starts with an action: send an email, place a call, move the case. The Decision Engine learns from how completed cases unfold and uses that evidence to choose the strongest next move for each open case.

The historical replay reads one morning snapshot per open case and estimates its likely path. The event-driven runtime being built reads the complete case history, determines where the case stands, removes moves that no longer fit, compares what remains, selects one, and dispatches it. When the evidence changes, the system reassesses the case immediately.

Historical replay tested four parts across 2.75 million case-days: the forecasts, the logic that reads each case, the rules that remove blocked moves, and the initial ranking of what remains. That ranking combines calibrated model scores with fixed, human-set priorities. The replay produced zero do-not-contact and hard-policy violations. It also found roughly 400,000 case-days when a response was already on record but another outreach attempt remained available. It did not execute the event-driven runtime; it tested the foundation that runtime will use.

The same discipline applies to the models. Three proposed improvements raised average accuracy but weakened the first page of the worklist, where the ranking matters most. The promotion gate rejected all three. A model is not better merely because its overall score improves. It must improve the part of the operation where its ranking will actually be used.

From event to execution — and back again

The architecture is a loop, and the return arrow matters more than any individual box.

The load-bearing rule sits in the middle: the registered play library defines what can exist, and deterministic constraints remove what does not fit. The prognosis models supply forecasts. The journey policy compares the remaining moves and selects one. A blocked play is removed, not penalized, so no forecast can restore it.

The Case Control Engine connects the case record, machine-learning forecasts, decision logic, and execution. It begins with patterns tested in historical replay. As completed cases accumulate, it learns which sequences produce the strongest results.

When the case changes, the plan changes

Use the illustrated guide’s fictional case 4127. A scheduled WAIT expires on day three and enters the case record. The state reducer rebuilds the current state. The prognosis models estimate the likely path. Fax disappears because no fax number exists; escalation disappears because the case has not reached that point. The initial policy selects email from the registered plays that remain, records why it outranked the alternatives, and dispatches it to the email system. Delivery becomes the next event.

The policy never ranks a flat menu of actions. Hard constraints remove the plays that do not fit; the policy compares what remains. A model or SLM cannot invent an unregistered play, and low predicted success alone cannot stop an unresolved case while an available play remains.

What the engine actually chooses

A play is not merely “send an email.” It is a complete, executable instruction: the objective, contact path, timing, content boundaries, executor, and the event that will bring the case back into the loop. The policy chooses that package; the SLM may write inside it, but it does not change the package.

What the specialist model fleet sees

The specialist fleet does not make one broad prediction. Different models answer different questions: which new cases are likely to verify, which open cases are beginning to stall, and which are likely to end unable to verify. Every specialist reads the same morning snapshot — one per open case per day, nearly three million across a twelve-month window of employment, education, reference, and license-style checks — and sees only what was knowable that morning. I chose gradient-boosted trees because this evidence is structured, the relationships are uneven, and the system needs probabilities that can be tested and calibrated, not merely labels.

In plain terms, the never-verify specialist places the eventual failure ahead of the eventual success 71% of the time, versus 50% for chance. The two-day and five-day specialists sort at 75% and 72%. At intake, when the system compares a case that will eventually fail with one that will verify, it ranks the future failure as harder approximately 81% of the time. Operationally, the result that matters is the spread: the easiest predicted tenth fails 3% of the time, while the hardest fails 48% of the time.

That 16× spread is also the business story. Today’s UTV rate treats cases with radically different odds as though they were equally winnable. Difficulty-adjusted measures separate the easy, hard, and recoverable middle so management can aim effort and judge outcomes more fairly.

A high score still cannot break a rule

The models estimate what is likely. The state reducer identifies what has already happened, what is blocked, and which moves still fit the case. The two jobs stay separate because they fail differently: a poor forecast can waste effort; a broken filter can create an incident. The state reducer classifies every case-day into one of thirteen work states — terminal, already-responded, policy-blocked, awaiting response, cooldown, review-due, research-needed, digital-paths-exhausted, standard-paths-exhausted, outreach-ready, and so on — and builds the set of viable moves from the state, never from a score. I expected the next win here to come from a better model. It came from a rule — the 400,000-case-day finding this post opened with, hiding in that third segment. The state reducer stops those already-responded case-days from advancing to another touch. No model was needed — only the definition, written down. The signal is a vendor receipt, not parsed reply content, and it is not available for every product; the count should be read within that boundary.

The same rule also shows why the safety result holds under pressure. Suppose the forecasts make another call look attractive on one of those cases. The state reducer sees that a reply is already on record, so call is removed before the journey policy compares the remaining moves. No forecast can restore it. That is why the replay produced zero do-not-contact and hard-policy violations: the rule checks run before selection, outside the models.

In historical replay, the system considered six possible actions per case-day — email, fax, call, online vendor, wait, escalate — removed the moves that did not fit the current state, and ordered the rest with calibrated model scores and fixed priorities. Across the full replay, that produced 16.5 million candidate actions. The product target replaces that initial ranking with the learned journey policy.

A seven-stage pipeline validates every handoff from candidates through the final queue; every stage checks its own output and halts the run on any failure, and a strict contract validator signs off at the end. In the completed run, no prohibited action reached rank one, every case group kept an available move, and the final queue passed end to end.

No peeking, by construction

The easiest way for a model like this to look brilliant is to let information from after the outcome leak into its features — post-outcome status fields, completion timestamps, identity proxies. Every feature here is built under strict “as-of” discipline instead: 117 columns, each computed only from facts recorded before the snapshot morning. An automated leakage audit re-checks that boundary on every new training matrix — and again at every monthly retrain. The scores in this post are conservative by design, measured the hard, honest way.

The system rejected three of my improvements

Each prediction target keeps its own incumbent model. A challenger must clear every criterion against that incumbent; the comparison and verdict are versioned, and a failed challenger stays out. The target autonomous lifecycle will extend that discipline: a qualifying action or content release will promote automatically, continuous monitoring will remain active, and rollback will restore the prior version.

The gate earns its keep on what it rejects. Three times this year I tried to improve the never-verify model — a settings-tuning pass, a guarded version of the same sweep, and this cycle’s note-reading variant — and the gate turned all three away for the same reason: each won on average and weakened the top of the ranked worklist, where accuracy is worth the most. One rejection is bad luck; three identical ones is a message. For this target, average accuracy and top-of-list sharpness genuinely pull against each other, so the next attempt changes the training data itself rather than pushing a fourth model variant.

Reading the notes without keeping them

The biggest accuracy gain this cycle came from the data source I had been most careful to avoid: the free-text notes attached to case history (“left a voicemail”; “reached HR, call back Tuesday”). Notes are where the richest signal lives — and where the risk lives twice over: outcome language (“verified via…”) leaks answers into training, and raw text carries the privacy exposure, so persisting it into training artifacts was never on the table.

The design that threads the needle: a deterministic reading layer classifies each note the moment it streams past — then throws the text away. Every note is sorted into eleven kinds of operational signal (contact made, voicemail left, callback promised, redirected to a third party, …), and only the signal survives: which kinds of events a case has seen, how recently, how often. Deterministic matters here — the same note always produces the same labels, so the privacy boundary has no black box in it and the whole layer can be audited and replayed. That turns 17.9 million unreadable notes into 30 new machine-usable signals per case-day for the learned models, computed under the same no-peeking time discipline as everything else; about 28% of case-days carry at least one.

The same new evidence improved the two success forecasts but weakened the top of the never-verify worklist. Two challengers advanced; the third was rejected.

One operational note survives from that build: a routine row-count check caught a corrupted data pull before any model consumed it. Cheap checks between stages are some of the highest-value code in the system.

What the evidence still will not let me say

The layers ahead are ambitious, which makes the boundary between built, ready, and not-yet-proved more important — not less. Three lines keep the story honest:

  • Historical replay is not live performance. The replay establishes that the system can read, route, and structure the work at scale. Live execution, cycle time, and operational outcomes remain to be measured.
  • No causal claims about channel, wording, or templates. Historical patterns like “email-first cases verified more often than call-first cases” are observational. Causal language stays banned until the live record connects the next move, action, template version, delivery or reply, and final outcome. The logging structure is built; the evidence is not yet collected.
  • Nothing auto-sends today. The event-driven runtime, automated digital actions, learned journey policy, controlled exploration, generated content, and automatic release process are being built now; they are not current production behavior.

The autonomous loop being built

Historical replay cannot prove that a particular channel, message, or follow-up caused a better result. That proof requires live records connecting each decision to the action taken and how the case ultimately ended.

The system follows one repeatable sequence: record what changed, update the case, remove blocked moves, forecast likely outcomes, choose one registered play, execute it, and use the result to reassess the case.

The next engineering requirement is a complete case record connecting every decision to the action taken and the result that followed. That record allows the engine to run the case now and learn how to run the next one better.

  • The engine learns from the full case: it learns which next move produces the strongest final result, including effects that become visible only later.
  • Controlled exploration is designed to create better evidence: the system can occasionally test a close alternative and record how likely each option was to be chosen — without ever testing a blocked move.
  • The content model learns separately: the journey policy chooses what to do; the SLM renders the structured explanation and generates the email, fax, or call script for the play already selected.
  • Execution closes the operational loop: digital plays run through machine adapters designed to prevent duplicate sends; calls and true exceptions enter one task list ranked across the entire caseload.
  • The release process closes the learning loop: completed cases train monthly challengers after outcomes have settled, qualifying releases promote automatically, and continuous monitoring can roll back to the prior version.

The architecture can extend to new customers, but the learning stays customer-aware. The same evidence structure, specialist-model design, and promotion process can be reused while predictions, policies, cadence, and language are recalibrated against each customer’s own outcomes. Learning should cross customer boundaries only where the evidence shows that the behavior transfers reliably.

Each component has one job. The case record preserves what happened. The state reducer updates what is true. Specialist models estimate where the case is heading. The decision policy chooses what to do. Execution acts and records the result. Completed cases improve the action and content policies.

The measured system today remains the disciplined foundation: calibrated prognosis, deterministic checks, mechanical model gates, and 2.75 million replayed case-days. The product being built closes the rest of the loop without changing the evidence standard. Events record. State updates. Constraints remove. Models forecast. The policy selects. Actions execute. Outcomes trigger a new decision and teach the next release. Restraint is still the launch mechanism, not the destination. ◆
